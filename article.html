Migrants' desperation outweighs fear at US southern border

Selwyn Allende was tired. With their one-year-old daughter on their shoulders and their pregnant wife, they had just sailed the Rio Grande from the Mexican city of Piedras Negras to Eagle Pass, Texas. The dangerous journey of thousands of migrants each year in search of a better future.

“I was so scared for my daughter in the river. "I feel tired and defeated, but I dream of having a job if the immigration authorities listen to us wholeheartedly," said the 30-year-old Guatemalan.

At his side, his wife, with a painful gesture, eyes closed, patrolling the border waiting for them under one of the two bridges that connect Mexico with the United States. His things are placed in many plastic bags.

Officers check their passports and those of other new arrivals and detain them to process their asylum claims. This scene was repeated several times a day before the unsuspecting gaze of the security forces. "No, it never ends. They can cross the border anytime, anywhere," said a National Guard soldier, who did not want to be replaced.

Tightening security in recent months has failed to stem the flow of visa-free immigrants. In May, authorities detained more than 239,000 people at the border with Mexico, a record number, although that number includes people who have repeatedly tried to enter the United States.

Texas Governor Approves State Forces to Detain Immigrants and Take Them to the Border

Texas Governor Greg Abbott allowed state officials to return to their port of entry to border with Mexican immigrants on Thursday. This can cause conflict with the federal government

The Republican Party has granted these powers to two state agencies, the National Guard and the Office of Public Safety (DPS), through an executive order.

According to Abbott, 5,000 illegal immigrants in the United States were arrested over the weekend, sparking a "border crisis that is overwhelming border communities."
